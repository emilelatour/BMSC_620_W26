Part 1: Why Not Just Use More t-Tests?

Where we’ve been


So far our inference toolkit for numerical outcomes has been:


Lesson Test Groups

10 Paired t-test 2 (matched)
11 Two-sample t-test 2 (independent)


Today’s question: What happens when we want to compare 3 or more groups?


The motivating question

A genetics study wants to know: does ACTN3 genotype (CC, CT, or TT) affect how much arm strength changes after an exercise intervention?

That’s three groups — we can’t use a two-sample t-test anymore.

Why not just run three t-tests?


With 3 groups (CC, CT, TT), we could run every possible pairwise comparison:


3 tests:

  • CC vs. CT
  • CC vs. TT
  • CT vs. TT


Each test has \(\alpha = 0.05\) probability of a Type I error.

The problem: With multiple tests, errors accumulate.

\[P(\text{at least one false positive}) = 1 - (1-\alpha)^m\]

For \(m = 3\) tests at \(\alpha = 0.05\):

\[1 - (0.95)^3 = 0.143\]

That’s a 14% chance of at least one false positive — not 5%!


The multiple comparisons problem

Running many tests inflates the Type I error rate beyond the nominal \(\alpha\). We need a method that tests all groups simultaneously — that’s ANOVA.

The multiple comparisons problem grows fast

If we had 4 groups that’s 6 tests → 19% chance of a false positive. ANOVA solves this by testing all groups in a single test.

Part 2: The Logic of ANOVA

Motivating example: arm strength and genotype


The FAMuSS Study

The Functional SNPs Associated with Muscle Size and Strength (FAMuSS) study investigated whether genetic variation is associated with exercise response.

  • Participants: ~600 young adults completed a resistance training program
  • Genetic variable: ACTN3 genotype at position R577X (CC, CT, or TT)
  • Outcome: Percent change in non-dominant arm strength after training

Question: Is mean percent change in arm strength different across the three genotype groups?

Mean arms strength by genotype group


library(oibiostat)
data("famuss")

famuss %>%
  group_by(actn3.r577x) %>%
  summarise(n = n(),
            mean = round(mean(ndrm.ch), 1),
            sd   = round(sd(ndrm.ch), 1))
# A tibble: 3 × 4
  actn3.r577x     n  mean    sd
  <fct>       <int> <dbl> <dbl>
1 CC            173  48.9  30  
2 CT            261  53.2  33.2
3 TT            161  58.1  35.7

Visualizing the three groups

The diamond ◆ marks the group mean. The dashed line is the grand mean across all participants. Do the groups look different enough to conclude a real effect?

The key question ANOVA asks


Whether or not group means appear different depends on two things:


1. How far apart are the group means?

If the group means are close to the grand mean → not much going on.

If the group means are far from the grand mean → evidence of a real difference.

This is between-group variability.

2. How much spread is there within each group?

If observations are tightly clustered around their group mean → easy to tell groups apart.

If observations are widely scattered → hard to tell groups apart even if means differ.

This is within-group variability.


ANOVA’s insight

  • ANOVA compares these two sources of variability.
  • If between-group variability is large relative to within-group variability, that’s evidence that the group means are genuinely different.

Three sources of variability — visually


\[\underbrace{SST}_{\text{Total}} = \underbrace{SSG}_{\text{Between groups}} + \underbrace{SSE}_{\text{Within groups}}\]

The ANOVA table


ANOVA organizes the variability into a table. \(k =\) the number of groups and \(N =\) the total number of observations across \(k\) groups:


Source SS df MS F
Between groups (Model) SSG \(k-1\) \(MSG = SSG/(k-1)\) \(F = MSG/MSE\)
Within groups (Error) SSE \(N-k\) \(MSE = SSE/(N-k)\)
Total SST \(N-1\)


Mean Squares: the key step

Dividing each SS by its degrees of freedom converts sums of squares into mean squares — averages of squared deviations. This puts between and within variability on the same scale so we can compare them with a ratio.

\[F = \frac{MSG}{MSE} = \frac{\text{between-group variability}}{\text{within-group variability}}\]

The F-statistic


\(F = \frac{MSG}{MSE}\)


When \(H_0\) is true (all means equal):

  • Between-group variability ≈ within-group variability
  • \(F \approx 1\)

When \(H_A\) is true (at least one mean differs):

  • Between-group variability > within-group variability
  • \(F > 1\), potentially much larger

Important

The p-value for an F-test is always the upper tail — we only care about F being surprisingly large.

Part 3: Running ANOVA in R

Step 1: State hypotheses


The ANOVA hypotheses

The null hypothesis for ANOVA is that all group means are equal:

\[H_0: \mu_{CC} = \mu_{CT} = \mu_{TT}\]

The alternative is that at least one pair of means differs:

\[H_A: \text{At least one mean differs from the others}\]


Common mistake

People often write \(H_A\) as “all means are different.” That is wrong!

\(H_A\) only requires that at least one group differs from at least one other. It could be that two groups are identical and a third is different — that still counts as \(H_A\).

Step 1: State hypotheses — in words


In symbols:


\[H_0: \mu_{CC} = \mu_{CT} = \mu_{TT}\]

\[H_A: \text{At least one mean differs from the others}\]

In words:


\(H_0\): The mean percent change in non-dominant arm strength is the same across all three ACTN3 genotype groups.

\(H_A\): The mean percent change in non-dominant arm strength differs for at least one pair of ACTN3 genotype groups.

Step 2: Set significance level


We use the conventional significance level:

\[\alpha = 0.05\]

Step 3: Check assumptions


The F-statistic follows an F-distribution when these conditions hold:


1. Independence: Observations within and across groups are independent

  • Met by design in FAMuSS (different participants in each group)


2. Normality within groups (or large samples):

  • Each group should be approximately normally distributed, OR \(n_i \geq 30\) per group
famuss %>%
  group_by(actn3.r577x) %>%
  summarise(n = n())
# A tibble: 3 × 2
  actn3.r577x     n
  <fct>       <int>
1 CC            173
2 CT            261
3 TT            161

All groups have \(n > 30\) ✓

Step 3: Check assumptions (continued)


3. Equal variance across groups (homoscedasticity):

  • Within-group variability should be similar across groups
  • Rule of thumb: violated if \(\max(s_i) / \min(s_i) > 2\)
famuss %>%
  group_by(actn3.r577x) %>%
  summarise(sd = sd(ndrm.ch)) %>%
  summarise(ratio = max(sd) / min(sd))
# A tibble: 1 × 1
  ratio
  <dbl>
1  1.19


Ratio is close to 1 — equal variance assumption is reasonable ✓

Tip

If the equal variance assumption is seriously violated, Welch’s ANOVA (oneway.test(..., var.equal = FALSE)) is a robust alternative that does not require equal variances.

Steps 4–5: Calculate F-statistic and p-value


# Fit the ANOVA model
famuss_aov <- aov(ndrm.ch ~ actn3.r577x, data = famuss)

# View the ANOVA table
summary(famuss_aov)
             Df Sum Sq Mean Sq F value Pr(>F)  
actn3.r577x   2   7043    3522   3.231 0.0402 *
Residuals   592 645293    1090                 
---
Signif. codes:  0 '***' 0.001 '**' 0.01 '*' 0.05 '.' 0.1 ' ' 1


Reading the output

  • actn3.r577x row = between-group (model) variability
  • Residuals row = within-group (error) variability
  • F value = MSG / MSE
  • Pr(>F) = p-value (upper tail of F-distribution)

Step 6: Conclusion


Recall our hypotheses:

\[H_0: \mu_{CC} = \mu_{CT} = \mu_{TT} \qquad \text{vs.} \qquad H_A: \text{At least one mean differs from the others}\]


With \(F\) = 3.23, \(df\) = (2, 592), p = 0.040, and \(\alpha = 0.05\): We reject \(H_0\).


Conclusion

There is sufficient evidence that the mean percent change in non-dominant arm strength differs for at least one pair of ACTN3 genotype groups (\(F\) = 3.23, \(df\) = 2, 592, p = 0.040).

Descriptively, mean arm strength change was 48.9% (SD = 30.0%) for CC, 53.2% (SD = 33.2%) for CT, and 58.1% (SD = 35.7%) for TT.

What ANOVA tells us — and what it doesn’t (1/2)


What ANOVA tells us — and what it doesn’t (2/2)


A critical limitation

ANOVA tells us that at least one pair of means differs. It does not tell us:

  • Which specific groups are different from which
  • How many pairs differ
  • The direction or magnitude of the differences

After a significant ANOVA, we need post-hoc tests to identify where the differences lie.

Part 4: Post-Hoc Comparisons

The multiple comparisons problem, revisited


After a significant ANOVA, we want to test all pairwise comparisons. With \(k = 3\) groups:

\[{3 \choose 2} = 3 \text{ pairwise tests: CC vs. CT, CC vs. TT, CT vs. TT}\]


If we just ran three regular t-tests at \(\alpha = 0.05\):

\[P(\text{at least one false positive}) = 1 - (0.95)^3 = 0.143\]


Post-hoc methods control this inflated error rate through different strategies — each with different tradeoffs between Type I and Type II error.

Post-hoc comparisons with rstatix


The rstatix package provides tidy post-hoc testing:


# Tukey HSD — controls family-wise error rate
famuss %>%
  tukey_hsd(ndrm.ch ~ actn3.r577x)
# A tibble: 3 × 9
  term  group1 group2 null.value estimate conf.low conf.high  p.adj p.adj.signif
* <chr> <chr>  <chr>       <dbl>    <dbl>    <dbl>     <dbl>  <dbl> <chr>       
1 actn… CC     CT              0     4.35   -3.25       12.0 0.371  ns          
2 actn… CC     TT              0     9.19    0.695      17.7 0.0303 *           
3 actn… CT     TT              0     4.83   -2.94       12.6 0.31   ns

Tukey’s Honest Significant Difference (HSD)


What it does:

  • Controls the family-wise error rate (FWER) — the probability of making any false positive across all comparisons
  • Compares all pairwise differences simultaneously
  • Reports both adjusted p-values and confidence intervals for each difference
  • The standard choice for ANOVA post-hoc testing

Which comparison is driving the significant ANOVA result?

Bonferroni correction

Strategy: Divide \(\alpha\) by the number of tests: \(\alpha_{Bonf} = \alpha / m\)

Equivalently, multiply each p-value by \(m\) and compare to \(\alpha\).

# Bonferroni correction
famuss %>%
  rstatix::pairwise_t_test(ndrm.ch ~ actn3.r577x,
                           p.adjust.method = "bonferroni")
# A tibble: 3 × 9
  .y.     group1 group2    n1    n2      p p.signif  p.adj p.adj.signif
* <chr>   <chr>  <chr>  <int> <int>  <dbl> <chr>     <dbl> <chr>       
1 ndrm.ch CC     CT       173   261 0.179  ns       0.537  ns          
2 ndrm.ch CC     TT       173   161 0.0113 *        0.0338 *           
3 ndrm.ch CT     TT       261   161 0.144  ns       0.433  ns


Bonferroni: pros and cons

Pro: Simple, transparent, very conservative (strong Type I error control)

Con: Loses power quickly as the number of comparisons grows — becomes hard to detect real differences

Bonferroni is best when you have a small number of a priori comparisons you care about.

False Discovery Rate (FDR)


Strategy: Control the expected proportion of false positives among significant results, rather than the probability of any false positive.

# Benjamini-Hochberg FDR
famuss %>%
  rstatix::pairwise_t_test(ndrm.ch ~ actn3.r577x,
                           p.adjust.method = "fdr")
# A tibble: 3 × 9
  .y.     group1 group2    n1    n2      p p.signif  p.adj p.adj.signif
* <chr>   <chr>  <chr>  <int> <int>  <dbl> <chr>     <dbl> <chr>       
1 ndrm.ch CC     CT       173   261 0.179  ns       0.179  ns          
2 ndrm.ch CC     TT       173   161 0.0113 *        0.0338 *           
3 ndrm.ch CT     TT       261   161 0.144  ns       0.179  ns


FDR: pros and cons

Pro: More powerful than Bonferroni or Tukey when there are many comparisons — allows more discoveries

Con: Some false positives are tolerated (you control the rate, not the occurrence)

FDR is especially popular in genomics and omics where hundreds or thousands of tests are run simultaneously.

Comparing the three approaches


Method Controls Best used when Conservativeness
Tukey HSD Family-wise error rate Post-ANOVA pairwise comparisons Moderate
Bonferroni Family-wise error rate Few a priori comparisons High
FDR False discovery rate Many simultaneous tests Low


Rule of thumb for BMSC 620

For ANOVA post-hoc testing in a standard biomedical study: Tukey HSD is your default.

Use Bonferroni when you have a small number of planned comparisons stated before looking at the data.

Use FDR when you’re doing many tests (e.g., comparing gene expression across dozens of groups).

Putting it all together: full reporting


A complete report of this analysis would include:


Example write-up

Mean percent change in non-dominant arm strength was 48.9% (SD = 30.0%) for CC, 53.2% (SD = 33.2%) for CT, and 58.1% (SD = 35.7%) for TT genotype groups. A one-way ANOVA indicated a statistically significant difference in mean arm strength change across genotype groups (\(F\)(2, 592) = 3.23, p = 0.040).

Post-hoc comparisons using Tukey’s HSD revealed that the TT group had significantly greater mean arm strength change than the CC group (difference = 9.2 percentage points, 95% CI: 0.7 to 17.7, adjusted p = 0.030). No significant differences were found between the CC and CT groups (adjusted p = 0.371) or the CT and TT groups (adjusted p = 0.310).


Note what’s included: group-level descriptive statistics, the F-statistic with both df values, the p-value, and specific post-hoc results with effect sizes and CIs.

Wrap-Up

ANOVA summary

When to use ANOVA:

  • Continuous outcome
  • 3 or more independent groups
  • Groups are independent of each other


The hypotheses:

\[H_0: \mu_1 = \mu_2 = \cdots = \mu_k\] \[H_A: \text{At least one mean differs from the others}\]


Key assumption to check:

  • Similar within-group SDs (\(SD_{max} / SD_{min}\) ratio < 2)
  • \(n \geq 30\) per group or approximate normality

R workflow:

# 1. Check assumptions
famuss %>%
  group_by(actn3.r577x) %>%
  summarise(n = n(), sd = sd(ndrm.ch))

# 2. Fit ANOVA
famuss_aov <- aov(ndrm.ch ~ actn3.r577x,
                  data = famuss)
summary(famuss_aov)

# 3. Post-hoc (if significant)
famuss %>%
  tukey_hsd(ndrm.ch ~ actn3.r577x)

Post-hoc methods:

tukey_hsd() Default for ANOVA
rstatix::pairwise_t_test
p.adjust = "bonferroni" Few planned comparisons
p.adjust = "fdr" Many tests

The big picture

ANOVA, t-tests, and linear regression are all part of the same family. The F-test in ANOVA and the t-test are mathematically related — in fact, for \(k = 2\) groups, \(F = t^2\).
