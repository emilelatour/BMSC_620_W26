Part 1: Parametric vs. Nonparametric

What do all our tests so far have in common?


Every test we’ve learned assumes the data come from a specific distribution:


Test Distributional assumption
One-sample t-test Normal population (or large \(n\))
Paired t-test Normal differences (or large \(n\))
Two-sample t-test Normal populations (or large \(n\))
ANOVA Normal within each group (or large \(n\))


These are called parametric tests — they assume the population can be described by specific parameters (\(\mu\), \(\sigma\)) under a named distribution.


What if the assumptions don’t hold?

Small samples + non-normal data + outliers = parametric tests may not be valid. Nonparametric tests are the answer.

When do parametric assumptions break down?


Situation 1: Small samples with skewed data

  • \(n = 8\) per group, right-skewed outcome
  • Central limit theorem doesn’t save you here
  • t-test p-values may not be reliable


Situation 2: Outliers that aren’t errors

  • Biological data often has extreme values
  • A single outlier can dominate the mean
  • The outlier is real — you can’t just remove it

Situation 3: Ordinal outcomes

  • Pain scale (1–10), severity scores, Likert items
  • Means aren’t always meaningful
  • “Average pain of 4.7” — what does that mean?


Situation 4: Heavy-tailed distributions

  • Gene expression, antibody titers, viral loads
  • Extreme values are common, not aberrant
  • Log-transformation helps, but isn’t always appropriate

The rank-based approach

Key idea: Instead of testing raw values, convert them to ranks first.


Step 1: Take your original data

Patient Viral load
A 125
B 3024
C 780
D 55
E 426

Step 2: Replace values with ranks

Patient Viral load Rank
D 55 1
A 125 2
E 426 3
C 780 4
B 3024 5


Why ranks work

Patient B’s viral load of 3024 is extreme, but as a rank it’s just “5” — the highest. Ranks compress outliers automatically. The rank-sum test uses these ranks instead of raw values, so no distributional assumption is needed.

Pros and cons of nonparametric tests

Advantages

  • Fewer assumptions — no normality required
  • Robust to outliers — extreme values become just another rank
  • Work with small samples — exact p-values available
  • Can handle ordinal data — ranks are meaningful for ordered categories

Trade-offs

  • Less powerful — converting to ranks discards information about magnitude
  • If parametric assumptions are met, the t-test or ANOVA will detect effects more efficiently
  • Confidence intervals are harder to compute
  • Ties in ranks require corrections


The bottom line

Use parametric tests when assumptions are met — they’re more powerful. Switch to nonparametric when you have small samples, severe skew, heavy outliers, or ordinal data.

The complete comparison table


Study design Parametric test Nonparametric alternative
Single sample, numeric One-sample t-test Wilcoxon signed-rank
Paired sample, numeric Paired t-test Wilcoxon signed-rank
Two independent samples, numeric Two-sample t-test Wilcoxon rank-sum
3+ independent samples, numeric ANOVA Kruskal-Wallis


What about categorical outcomes?

For 2×2 tables with small expected cell counts, we use Fisher’s exact test — we covered this already in Lesson 14. It’s the nonparametric alternative to the chi-squared test for categorical data.

Part 2: Wilcoxon Signed-Rank Test

First: the sign test (brief)


The sign test is the simplest nonparametric test for paired or single-sample data.


Paired setting: each subject \(i\) has two measurements — a first observation \(x_{i1}\) (e.g., pre-treatment) and a second observation \(x_{i2}\) (e.g., post-treatment). Compute the within-subject difference:

\[d_i = x_{i2} - x_{i1}\]

Single-sample setting: each subject has one observation \(x_i\), and we compare it against a hypothesized value \(\mu_0\):

\[d_i = x_i - \mu_0\]


In both cases, the test proceeds the same way:

  • Count the number of positive differences (\(D^+\)) and negative differences (\(D^-\))
  • Ignore ties (\(d_i = 0\))
  • Under \(H_0\): differences are symmetric around 0, so \(D^+ \sim \text{Binomial}(n, 0.5)\)


Why we don’t use the sign test


The problem with the sign test

The sign test only uses the direction of each difference — not the size.

A difference of 0.01 and a difference of 10.0 both count as a single “+”. This throws away most of the information in your data and makes the test much less powerful than it could be.

The Wilcoxon signed-rank test solves this by incorporating the magnitude of differences through ranking.

The Wilcoxon signed-rank test


The upgrade over the sign test: We rank the absolute values of the differences, then use the signs.


Conceptual steps:

  1. Compute differences \(d_i\) (drop any zeros)
  2. Rank the absolute values \(|d_i|\) from smallest to largest (ties get average ranks)
  3. Attach the original sign (+/−) back to each rank
  4. Sum the positive ranks (\(T^+\)) and negative ranks (\(T^-\))
  5. If \(H_0\) is true, \(T^+\) and \(T^-\) should be roughly equal


What’s being tested

\(H_0\): The population of differences is symmetric around zero

\(H_A\): The population of differences is not symmetric around zero

This is equivalent to testing whether the median difference is zero — provided the distribution is symmetric.

The glaucoma example

Intraocular pressure in glaucoma patients is often reduced by treatment with adrenaline. A new synthetic drug is being evaluated — more expensive, but potentially more effective.

  • 7 patients treated with both drugs (one eye each)
  • Outcome: reduction in pressure after treatment
  • Larger values = greater pressure reduction

Question: Do the two drugs produce the same reduction in pressure?

Step 1: State hypotheses


\(H_0\): The differences in intraocular pressure reduction (synthetic − adrenaline) are symmetrically distributed around zero

\(H_A\): The differences are not symmetrically distributed around zero


In plain language:

\(H_0\): The two drugs produce the same median reduction in intraocular pressure

\(H_A\): The two drugs differ in median reduction of intraocular pressure

Step 2: Set significance level

\[\alpha = 0.05\]

Step 3: Check assumptions


Wilcoxon signed-rank assumptions:

  1. Paired observations (✓ — same patients, one eye each)
  2. Differences are continuous (✓ — pressure measurements)
  3. Distribution of differences is symmetric around the median (check visually)


  • Assumptions 1 and 2 are verified from the study design alone.
  • For assumption 3, we plot the distribution of differences \(d_i\) and look for approximate symmetry around zero — no need for perfect normality, just no severe skew or outliers pulling in one direction.

Step 3: Check assumptions — visually


What to look for: approximate symmetry around zero — no severe skew, no outliers pulling strongly in one direction.


What we see:

  • Most differences are positive — the synthetic drug tends to reduce pressure more
  • The spread on both sides of zero is roughly balanced
  • With a small sample, we do not assume normality — only approximate symmetry


✓ Assumption 3 is satisfied.

How ranking works — conceptually

1 3.5 3.2 -0.3 0.3 1.5 -1.5
2 2.6 3.1 0.5 0.5 4.5 4.5
3 3.0 3.3 0.3 0.3 1.5 1.5
4 1.9 2.4 0.5 0.5 4.5 4.5

  • Patient 5 had \(d = 0\) → dropped from analysis (effective \(n = 6\))
  • \(T^+ = 1.5 + 3 + 4.5 + 4.5 + 6 = 19.5\) (sum of positive ranks)
  • \(T^- = 1.5\) (sum of negative ranks, in absolute value)
  • If \(H_0\) true: \(T^+\) and \(T^-\) should be roughly equal

  • statistic = \(T^+\) = 19.50 (sum of positive ranks)

Step 6: Conclusion


With p = 0.073 and \(\alpha = 0.05\), we fail to reject \(H_0\).


Conclusion

There is insufficient evidence that the median difference in intraocular pressure reduction between the synthetic drug and adrenaline differs from zero (Wilcoxon signed-rank test, \(T^+\) = 19.50, p = 0.073).

The synthetic drug showed a numerically higher median reduction (difference = 0.40 mmHg), but this difference was not statistically significant at \(\alpha = 0.05\) with only 6 evaluable patients. A larger study would be needed to draw a definitive conclusion.


Note on sample size

\(n = 7\) (effective \(n = 6\)) gives this study very low power. Failing to reject \(H_0\) here does not mean the drugs are equivalent — it may simply mean we don’t have enough data.

Part 3: Wilcoxon Rank-Sum Test

The nonparametric two-sample t-test


When the two-sample t-test assumptions don’t hold and the groups are independent, use the Wilcoxon rank-sum test (also called the Mann-Whitney U test).


The idea:

  1. Combine both groups into one dataset
  2. Rank all observations from 1 to \(N\)
  3. Sum the ranks within each group
  4. If the groups have the same median, the rank sums should be roughly equal
  5. If one group tends to have higher ranks, that group has a higher median


Hypotheses

\(H_0\): The distributions of the two populations are identical

\(H_A\): The distributions of the two populations are not identical

(When both groups share the same shape, any difference reflects a shift in location — i.e., a difference in median.)

Part 4: Kruskal-Wallis Test

The nonparametric ANOVA

When ANOVA assumptions fail with 3 or more groups, use the Kruskal-Wallis test.


It extends the rank-sum test to \(k\) groups:

  1. Combine all groups, rank all observations 1 to \(N\)
  2. Compute the sum of ranks within each group: \(R_1, R_2, \ldots, R_k\)
  3. If \(H_0\) is true, rank sums should be similar across groups
  4. The test statistic \(H\) quantifies how different the rank sums are

\[H_0: \text{The distributions of all } k \text{ groups are identical}\] \[H_A: \text{At least one group has a different distribution}\]


Tip

The K-W test statistic \(H\) follows a \(\chi^2\) distribution with \(k - 1\) degrees of freedom. When \(k = 2\), K-W gives the same result as the rank-sum test.

When all groups share the same distributional shape, any difference reflects a shift in location — i.e., a difference in median.

Tip

There is no nonparametric equivalent of Tukey HSD. Bonferroni correction is a straightforward choice — it controls the family-wise error rate across all pairwise comparisons. With only 3 pairs here, the penalty for using Bonferroni is small.

Wrap-Up

Choosing the right nonparametric test


Question Test
Is the median different from a value? (single sample) Wilcoxon signed-rank
Do paired measurements differ? Wilcoxon signed-rank
Do two independent groups differ? Wilcoxon rank-sum
Do 3+ independent groups differ? Kruskal-Wallis


Key decision rule:

  1. Are parametric assumptions met? → Use parametric test (more power)
  2. Small \(n\), skewed data, outliers, ordinal outcome? → Use nonparametric test

R functions summary:

# Signed-rank (paired / one-sample)
wilcox.test(data$d, mu = 0)

# Rank-sum (two independent groups)
wilcox.test(outcome ~ group, 
            data = data)

# Kruskal-Wallis (3+ groups)
kruskal.test(outcome ~ group, 
             data = data)

# Post-hoc after K-W
pairwise.wilcox.test(
  data$outcome, data$group,
  p.adjust.method = "bonferroni"
  )

The big picture

Nonparametric tests are a safety net — they work when parametric assumptions fail. But they’re not magic. If assumptions are met, stick with t-tests and ANOVA. If they aren’t, you now have the right tools to handle it.
